The Grand Exhibition of Modern Wonders Draws Record Crowds to the Capital

Thousands flock to marvel at flying machines, electric contraptions, and the extraordinary phonographic orchestra

BY ojurereoluwa o. ogunmilua

·

STAFF CORRESPONDENT

·

MAY 16, 1926

🏛️

The Grand Exhibition Hall, as seen from the East Promenade

Fig. I — Illustration by our artist, Mr. Reginald Pemberton

he vast halls of the Crystal Pavilion were thrown open to the public yesterday morning, and by all accounts the spectacle within surpassed even the most extravagant predictions of its organisers. Crowds began assembling before dawn, their breath misting in the cool morning air as they jostled patiently for admittance.

Among the exhibits drawing the greatest fascination was the remarkable aeroplane constructed entirely of aluminium by Messrs. Hawthorne & Sons of Birmingham, which was demonstrated in the outer courtyard to the accompaniment of tremendous applause. Several members of our party declared it the most extraordinary sight they had ever witnessed.

The phonographic orchestra, conducted by the celebrated Maestro Floriani, performed selections from Verdi and Schubert through a remarkable system of amplifying horns, filling the rotunda with music of astonishing clarity. Ladies in attendance were observed pressing handkerchiefs to their eyes during the more affecting passages.

We understand that Her Majesty's representative, the Honourable Sir Edward Colquhoun, will formally open the exhibition on the morrow, after which the public shall be admitted at a reduced tariff of thruppence per person, sixpence for families.

"A triumph of human ingenuity — the age of marvels is upon us at last"

— SIR EDWARD COLQUHOUN, ROYAL CORRESPONDENT
